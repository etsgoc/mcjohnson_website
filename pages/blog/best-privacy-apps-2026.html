The Best Privacy Apps for 2026

Tools that actually protect your data — tested and used daily.

🔒

Privacy apps have gotten dramatically better over the past two years. The ecosystem has matured, the defaults have improved, and the excuses for not protecting your data have gotten thinner. Here's what I actually run on my devices — not a sponsored list, just what I trust.

💡 Everything here is something I personally use. Affiliate links are marked with *, meaning I earn a small commission if you buy through them — at no extra cost to you.

Password Management

If you're still reusing passwords, this is the one thing to fix first. A good password manager removes friction entirely — it generates, stores, and fills passwords across all your devices without you thinking about it.

🔐

1Password

The best password manager I've used. Works seamlessly across Apple devices, integrates with Touch ID and Face ID, and the UI is genuinely good — which matters when you use it 20 times a day.

VPN

I don't run a VPN 24/7, but I always use one on public networks — airports, hotels, cafes. The risk of a misconfigured network is real, and a VPN is cheap insurance.

🛡️

NordVPN

Fast, reliable, and covers up to 10 devices on one account. I've tried a few VPNs over the years — NordVPN is the one I kept. No-logs policy, good jurisdiction, and the kill switch actually works.

Private Networking

This one is more for developers but worth mentioning. Tailscale lets you connect all your devices on a private network without a central server. Free for personal use and genuinely zero-config.

🌐

Tailscale

Zero-config mesh VPN. I use it to securely access my home machine from anywhere without opening ports. Free tier is more than enough for personal use.

Most finance apps sync your data to their servers — which means your income, spending habits, and bank details live on someone else's computer. That always bothered me, which is part of why I built Pefi.

The Honest Summary

You don't need to run all of these. Start with a password manager — that alone removes 80% of the risk most people face. Add a VPN if you use public networks regularly. The rest is incremental improvement.

Privacy doesn't require paranoia. It just requires making slightly better decisions about which apps handle your data and where that data lives. The tools above make those decisions easy.
