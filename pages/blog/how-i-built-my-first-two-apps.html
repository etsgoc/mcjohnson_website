How I Built My First Two Apps as a Solo Developer

From idea to launch — the real process behind Pefi and Yearbook.

Building your first app is one thing. Building two — and actually shipping them — is a completely different challenge.

Over the past year, I built Pefi and Yearbook as a solo developer. No team, no external funding, no agency help. Just me, a set of tools, and a lot of iteration. This isn't a "how to get rich building apps" story. It's a breakdown of what actually goes into shipping real products alone — and what I'd do differently.

The Reality of Building Solo

The hardest part isn't coding. It's decision-making.

  • What features actually matter
  • What to cut (even when it hurts)
  • When something is "good enough" to ship

As a solo developer, every hour matters. You don't get to hide behind complexity — you have to simplify everything.

Step 1 — Start With a Problem You Actually Understand

Both apps came from personal friction.

Pefi came from frustration with finance apps storing sensitive data in the cloud. I wanted something local, private, and simple — where your data stays on your device and nowhere else.

Yearbook came from a completely different place — capturing memories and structuring them in a way that feels intentional instead of chaotic. A 365-day view of your year, with journals, routines, and memories that belong to you.

No market research. No surveys. Just problems I personally cared about solving. That matters more than people think.

Step 2 — Design Before You Build

I used Figma for all UI/UX work. Not because it's trendy — but because it forces clarity.

Before writing a single line of code, I mapped core screens, user flows, and edge cases. This saved weeks of rework. A simple rule: if the design feels confusing in Figma, it will feel worse in code.

Step 3 — Build Once, Ship Everywhere

For development, I used Expo. This let me write one codebase and ship to both iOS and Android without managing native complexity. For a solo developer, speed is leverage. You're not optimizing for perfect performance — you're optimizing for momentum.

Step 4 — Use AI as a Multiplier, Not a Crutch

AI didn't build my apps — but it accelerated everything. I used it to debug faster, generate boilerplate code, and explore alternative approaches when I was stuck.

The key is knowing what not to trust blindly. AI works best when you already understand the problem. Otherwise you're just copying solutions you don't fully control.

Step 5 — Payments Without Friction

For monetization, I used RevenueCat. Handling subscriptions manually across platforms is painful — RevenueCat abstracts in-app purchases, subscription states, and cross-platform syncing. This let me focus on the product instead of billing logic.

Step 6 — Build Less Than You Want

This is where most solo developers fail. You don't need 10 features, complex onboarding, or edge-case handling for everything. You need one clear use case, a smooth core experience, and stability. Everything else comes later — if it's needed.

Biggest Mistakes I Made

  • Overbuilding early versions with features nobody asked for
  • Delaying launch for "one more feature" that kept moving
  • Underestimating how much polish the final 10% requires

Shipping teaches more than planning ever will.

What Actually Worked

  • Keeping scope tight from day one
  • Designing first, coding second
  • Using tools that reduce friction rather than add capability
  • Treating privacy as a feature, not an afterthought

You don't need a team to build something meaningful. You need clarity, consistency, and the willingness to ship before you feel ready. The first version won't be perfect. It's not supposed to be.

Both apps are now live on the App Store. If you're building something similar — or thinking about starting — feel free to reach out. Happy to share what worked and what I'd skip next time.
